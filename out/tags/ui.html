Clear, gesture controlled lists

The app store is full of to-do apps, but Clear is different. Its UI has many interesting intuitions and looks fresh and inspired.
While early touch based interfaces relied heavily on buttons, new concepts surface exploring the power of gestures.
Gestures are not the only answer to interface challenges, but they are surely underused.
It is important to remember that serious drawbacks do exist: first and foremost, gestures require learning to get accustomed to. Also, some gestures potentially overlap with other system features (iPhone's swipe to delete comes to mind, as it overlaps with the rampant use of horizontal sliding card metaphor that Twitter for iPad made famous).

Still, I think gestures are a powerful way to let users explore and manipulate the space of an app. And Clear shows interesting new interactions.

Zephyr, multitasking gestures for iPhone

iOS is a modern mobile operating system, still - especially on the iPhone - doesn't make heavy use of gestures. Zephyr could change that.

Windows 8: Ribbon Explorer

With Windows 8, Microsoft is rethinking its desktop OS. Explorer, the file manager, is one of the key components, as it represents one of the most used parts of the system. Microsoft approached the design of this element with a data informed approach, based on telemetry data recorded from real users (and this means millions of sessions).
The file manager is not one of the sexiest parts of an OS, but it is a very core one. Microsoft's approach with it is quite conservative: win8 Explorer is not a transition to something new. It implements solid ribbon concepts, in a different context.
Be sure to check out the interesting post about it that was featured on win8 development blog.

Reshaping e-book Interfaces

The iPad helped e-books become mainstream, but even now with tablet computers, there is still much room for improvement on the interface. The Kaist Institute of Technology created a remarkable prototype, introducing some concepts that define a new level of interaction for ebooks on a touch screen device.

Getting Out of the Building

Google's Tomer Sharon has some precious hints on how to approach user research.

The 25 minutes talk is rich in useful suggestions, some of which are not as commonly accepted as they should be. One of my favorite parts is the "if you are spending $50,000 on a pre-launch usability test you are probably doing it wrong" quote. User research doesn't require fancy budgets - and most of the time fancy budgets subtract value from user research instead of adding it.

Tomer talks about simple techniques you can use for your research, with the goal to get out of the building and see things from the perspective of your users. Throughout your journey, one of the key aspects will be getting buy-in for your research. And key will be making the whole team part of the research: it's the best way for everyone to understand the value of it and participate in making a better product.

If you find the video interesting, be sure to check out Tomer's new book "It's our research" and follow him on twitter @tsharon

A better iPad Keyboard

The Ipad has been considered a consumption device since its launch in 2010, but things are slowing starting to change. New apps are redefining what can be done on a tablet - iPhoto being one of the best examples in this area. From a UX point of view, gestures are enabling a powerful paradigm shift.
One of the missing pieces is still a better typing experience. There is something promising on the horizon, as this video shows us how a simple tweak could greatly increase software keyboard usability.
The idea is simple: swiping with one or two fingers on the keyboard lets you move the cursor, consequently speeding up text editing. Combining this simple gesture with the shift key also improves the text selection experience.
This technique is known as the Hooper Selection, after the name of the Georgia Tech student who conceived it.
In a few weeks several implementations came to life and jailbreakers can try SwipeSelection on Cydia right now. I really think this is a better way of dealing with text on tablet devices and hope the feature will see the light in future versions of iOS.

Firefox Junior iPad Browser

Mozilla tries to re-imagine browsing on the iPad with Junior, a prototype with a couple of interesting concepts. First of all, the interface gets rid of tabs: removing the chrome makes the browsing experience more immersive. Second, they introduce a couple of layered controls, for the back button and for other functions (like tab management).
The video is a little long and is not a polished product presentation, but is worth watching.
Mozilla's approach is obviously not the only attempt at building more immersive browsing experiences: Dolphin HD - among others - implements full screen browsing in a nice way, with gestures and spatial controls (swipe left and right to reveal interface elements).

Frog Reimagines Android UI with Feel_UX

Frog Design collaborates with Sharp to deliver an innovative Android skin based on Ice Cream Sandwich, called feel_UX. The design extensively focused on the lock screen, that becomes more personal and interactive. Another big change is on the home screen, divided in 3 columns - apps, widgets and shortcuts - making it easier for novice users to find their way around the OS.
This series of Aquos smartphones will be available in Japan this Summer.

A Whole New Mailbox

Gesture based interfaces look very attractive: they need a minimum amount of on-screen objects, often resulting in uncluttered interfaces.
The down side is that they are much harder to use in every day life than they look on presentation videos.

Still, there are a couple of concepts I really like, because they fit with the way I deal with my email:

  • immediately actionable message feed: mails are treated like a todo list. I usually accomplish this setup marking as unread the mails I need to act on, while forgetting about all the others. Mailbox's interface is perfectly suited to this kind of use, even letting you reorder the message stream
  • message snoozing: this is something I really miss in my current mail clients, and I can see myself using this feature a lot.

Leap Motion

Are gestures interfaces ready for prime time? We will find out in May, when Leap Motion launches.

Leap Motion is set to bridge the gap between camera-based controllers (like Kinect) and touchscreens: it is a tiny device that senses how you move your hands and fingers and lets you control your pc or Mac.
The device will start shipping in May - with developers already at work on the first units - and then we wil see if gestures are ready for prime time. Touchscreens on traditional desktop pcs have failed so far: the interaction feels unnatural and strain is a major issue when you spend long hours in front of a computer.

From Fingers to Armband

MYO armband promises to make sci-fi scenarios come true, using both your arms movements and electrical activity from your muscles to engage with digital interfaces.

The mouse is slowing fading away as the de facto standard in interacting with digital interfaces. Gestures are taking over and there is incredible innovation to be seen in this field.
Touch screens ignited the revolution, then new approaches started to surface in late 2012. Leap Motion found a smart way to bridge the gap between Microsoft's Kinect and touchscreens, in a way that elegantly works also for the good old pcs.
MYO armband tries to push this concept to the next level: the user actually wears the controller, hands-free. The product is still in development, with first units scheduled for release in late 2013, and demo videos reflect more of the makers vision than actual capabilities.
While it's easy to envision the possibilities this scenario opens, it is also interesting to think about potential drawbacks that wearable controllers would imply. The very first thing that comes to mind is preventing unwanted input: MYO will feature a unique gesture to be turned on and off. However, considering how many sudden activities we perform with our arms, a smart way to differentiate between input and noise will be key to the success of such devices.
