Firefox Junior iPad Browser

Mozilla tries to re-imagine browsing on the iPad with Junior, a prototype with a couple of interesting concepts. First of all, the interface gets rid of tabs: removing the chrome makes the browsing experience more immersive. Second, they introduce a couple of layered controls, for the back button and for other functions (like tab management).
The video is a little long and is not a polished product presentation, but is worth watching.
Mozilla's approach is obviously not the only attempt at building more immersive browsing experiences: Dolphin HD - among others - implements full screen browsing in a nice way, with gestures and spatial controls (swipe left and right to reveal interface elements).

Frog Reimagines Android UI with Feel_UX

Frog Design collaborates with Sharp to deliver an innovative Android skin based on Ice Cream Sandwich, called feel_UX. The design extensively focused on the lock screen, that becomes more personal and interactive. Another big change is on the home screen, divided in 3 columns - apps, widgets and shortcuts - making it easier for novice users to find their way around the OS.
This series of Aquos smartphones will be available in Japan this Summer.

A Whole New Mailbox

Gesture based interfaces look very attractive: they need a minimum amount of on-screen objects, often resulting in uncluttered interfaces.
The down side is that they are much harder to use in every day life than they look on presentation videos.

Still, there are a couple of concepts I really like, because they fit with the way I deal with my email:

  • immediately actionable message feed: mails are treated like a todo list. I usually accomplish this setup marking as unread the mails I need to act on, while forgetting about all the others. Mailbox's interface is perfectly suited to this kind of use, even letting you reorder the message stream
  • message snoozing: this is something I really miss in my current mail clients, and I can see myself using this feature a lot.

Leap Motion

Are gestures interfaces ready for prime time? We will find out in May, when Leap Motion launches.

Leap Motion is set to bridge the gap between camera-based controllers (like Kinect) and touchscreens: it is a tiny device that senses how you move your hands and fingers and lets you control your pc or Mac.
The device will start shipping in May - with developers already at work on the first units - and then we wil see if gestures are ready for prime time. Touchscreens on traditional desktop pcs have failed so far: the interaction feels unnatural and strain is a major issue when you spend long hours in front of a computer.

From Fingers to Armband

MYO armband promises to make sci-fi scenarios come true, using both your arms movements and electrical activity from your muscles to engage with digital interfaces.

The mouse is slowing fading away as the de facto standard in interacting with digital interfaces. Gestures are taking over and there is incredible innovation to be seen in this field.
Touch screens ignited the revolution, then new approaches started to surface in late 2012. Leap Motion found a smart way to bridge the gap between Microsoft's Kinect and touchscreens, in a way that elegantly works also for the good old pcs.
MYO armband tries to push this concept to the next level: the user actually wears the controller, hands-free. The product is still in development, with first units scheduled for release in late 2013, and demo videos reflect more of the makers vision than actual capabilities.
While it's easy to envision the possibilities this scenario opens, it is also interesting to think about potential drawbacks that wearable controllers would imply. The very first thing that comes to mind is preventing unwanted input: MYO will feature a unique gesture to be turned on and off. However, considering how many sudden activities we perform with our arms, a smart way to differentiate between input and noise will be key to the success of such devices.
